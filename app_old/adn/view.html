What Is the Difference Between Heroin Tolerance and Heroin Dependence?

Tolerance and dependence are two very different things. Tolerance develops similarly in many types of addiction. People develop tolerances to the effects of heroin or other substances if they use these substances regularly. So, if you develop a tolerance to heroin, the more you use heroin, the more you will have to use to get the same effects. When you abuse heroin, you might never feel as good as the first time you used it. When you use anytime after that, you are chasing that initial feeling and feeding your growing addiction. A dependence to drugs occurs when people who use heroin or other drugs begin to feel that they need to use heroin or other substances in order to function normally. When people who are dependent on heroin stop using the drug, they will frequently experience heroin withdrawal symptoms such as:
  • Nausea
  • Abdominal pain
  • Sweating
  • Shaking
  • Nervousness
  • Agitation
  • Depression
  • Muscle spasms
  • Cravings for drugs
  • Relapse
  • Flu-like symptoms
Constantly using and withdrawing from heroin can be painful and dangerous. These behaviors create a gruesome cycle that can be very hard to break. As people continue to use heroin for long periods of time, their tolerance for the drug increases. With that increase in tolerance also comes longer and more severe heroin withdrawal symptoms. A good way to break this cycle is to seek help from a heroin treatment program at a rehab center. This rehab treatment center can help you detox from heroin and rehabilitate you back into society.

The Heroin Treatment Process

The Intake Process at Rehabs

Heroin rehab treatment typically starts with an intake appointment at a treatment center. At this time, you will sign formal consent forms for your rehab treatment and staff members will explain the program rules to you. During the intake process at rehabs, the staff will also ask you questions about your physical and mental health and your substance abuse history. Sometimes they will give you a physical exam to determine your general health. After the intake assessment at rehabs, counselors or case managers and other professionals will draft your rehab treatment programs, including estimates on how long your rehab stay might be. You can ask any questions you may have about heroin rehab treatment and your treatment center at this time.

Heroin Detox at Rehabs

What is it really like to withdraw from heroin? Heroin detox can be a very uncomfortable process. While the withdrawal symptoms usually cannot kill you, many people at rehabs have reported that they feel like they were dying during this time. Detox programs at rehabs can be physically and psychologically stressful. Many drug treatment rehabs have heroin detox programs that are designed to help you wean off the drug and relieve withdrawal symptoms, all while your body adjusts to functioning without heroin or other drugs. You should thus complete heroin detox and other forms of rehab treatment with medical supervision. Heroin detox programs are different depending on which treatment program or rehab center you choose. Some rehab facilities use an on-site medical-assisted program. A medical detox rehab program provides patients with tapering doses of replacement medications such as Suboxone, buprenophine, naloxone, or methadone. Such drugs can help reduce the painful symptoms of heroin withdrawal in the safe and medically monitored settings of rehabs. The other type of detox is an abstinence-only or social detox. An abstinence-only detox program does not use replacement drugs. Instead, this program requires people to stop using heroin without any major medical assistance from rehabs or other sources. Many people find this detox treatment program quite challenging due the painful nature of heroin detox. Because of this, using medication-assisted treatment (MAT) in the detox process is a common way many rehabs manage heroin dependence.

Therapy at Rehabs

After detox, the hard part of heroin rehab treatment is over, right? Not quite. While finishing detox is a significant accomplishment, people who have achieved this milestone at rehabs still have work to do at rehabs and on their own. That is because the physical agony of heroin withdrawal symptoms might last days, but the psychological withdrawal symptoms might last a lot longer. As a consequence, after the detox stage is over, many people enter rehab addiction therapy as the next phase of their rehab treatment. There are many different program options for therapy at heroin treatment facilities. Most rehabs include individual and group therapy and offer general education about addiction. Since heroin addiction is often considered a family disease, many heroin rehabs incorporate family counseling sessions. Rehabs have found that heroin addiction often coexists with a dual diagnosis. Dual diagnosis is the term professionals use when a person has a mental illness such as depression or bipolar disorder along with a problem with addiction. If you or a loved one is struggling with a dual diagnosis, seeking rehab treatment facilities that specialize in dual diagnosis care makes sense, since such treatment facilities use tactics that address both heroin addiction and mental illness.

Heroin addiction and drug use in general are disorders that have no known cure. Recovery is possible and very achievable but it is a lifelong process. Some say that the real recovery starts outside of rehab treatment, so listen to the professionals at your heroin treatment facilities. If such rehabs believe that you need aftercare, it is something to definitely consider. (Aftercare is continuing care and other help after people leave rehab.) Drug rehab treatment gives you the tools to live life without the aid of drugs or alcohol. For addicts who have just left their rehabs, this might seem impossible at first. Aftercare at rehabs, though, provides the tools people can use to abstain from drugs. During the treatment process at a rehab center, trained counselors work with each individual to develop an aftercare plan. Aftercare options include outpatient treatment, private therapy, 12-step support group meetings such as AA and NA, long-term residential treatment, and sober living housing.

Interventions

We have all seen the television show Intervention and other depictions of interventions. Most people have heard of interventions, but what exactly are they? Interventions are meetings where the participants aim to convince someone to enter rehab treatment for a heroin addiction or other substance abuse problem. A trained interventionist or other professional typically directs such meetings. During interventions, close family members and friends express their love and concern for the person who is abusing drugs or alcohol. The family and friends also vocalize how the individual’s addiction has affected them and typically use such meetings to discuss rehabs and other treatment program options with their loved one.
